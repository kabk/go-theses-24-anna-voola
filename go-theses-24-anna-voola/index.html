Constrain, Contain, Complain

This thesis explores the transformative power of storytelling, using mouldy bread as an analogy to describe the power and entanglement of storytelling. Stories, just alike an airborne fungus in bread, infiltrate the greater narrative of the world, influencing the discourse and shaping perceptions. Focused on the overlooked narratives of the female experience, this thesis challenges the dominance of the white cis male-dominated stories. Further, it explores methods within the realm of literature and fiction, to share experiences by the unwanted and suppressed voices through history and society.



The concept of the evolving archive, similar to the mouldy bread or an unopened Tupperware, challenges conventional perceptions of archives as being rigid and controlled spaces. This thesis proposes a shift towards healing inequalities, emphasizing the importance of embracing diverse stories. Ultimately, storytelling emerges as a dynamic tool for rewriting and detangling the master narrative, to then hopefully become a more inclusive world where every voice contributes to our shared memories and narratives.

The image description

I once heard, that if your bread has at one spot, you can’t just scrape it — or cut it — away, like you would do with your cheese or vegetables. You would have to throw the entire loaf of bread away, even though the rest of it hasn’t been touched by the mould at all.

Since the mould apparently is airborne and bread being rather porous the mould would travel incredibly fast through the entire body of the loaf, and by the time you notice the fuzzy, green, and white spores in the corner of your bread, the microscopic threads of the fungi will already have woven itself into the entire loaf. And by now it would be far too late to even think about eating a single crumb of the loaf of bread.



In many ways I see stories to be just like that As a story is being told, being brought to life, and in some way being planted in this world — no matter being the bedtime stories told from the side of the bed, or it being incredible literary work about the female experience — it immediately infiltrates the greater narrative of the world. It travels into the gaps of the untoldness and fills it up with new knowledge and perceptions.

You might not be able to see it, or feel it instantly, but the threads of the story are going to affect the discourse, and make the world interconnected across events and experiences.

This might sound a bit farfetched at this point, but this is the power that storytelling contains, the power to change and shake up our perception of the world — no matter the story being big or small — it can (or will) eventually lead to a change. Storytelling protects and maintains endlessness and ongoingness of the world, and for everyday passing, more and more stories come to life. The threads of the stories tangle itself into the empty spaces of the greater narrative.

This makes it rather impossible to cover every aspect of the tool of storytelling and therefore in this thesis, my exploring and curiosity stems from an interest in speculative fabulation, speculative feminism, science fiction — or just SF, which is a term I borrowed from professor and ecofeminist, Donna Haraway. Haraway argues that none of these listed genres can exist without each other and goes on to explain how SF is a way of tracing the threads. To find the tangles and patterns, to pass on and to receive, to make and to unmake, and to pick up the threads and to drop them again.(Haraway, Staying with the Trouble: Making Kin in the Chthulucene)

The urge and motivation for this theme is to investigate the entanglement of narratives — more specific the expression of female experiences — and to explore what occurs when the threads of stories interconnects and how this has contributed to the shaping of the world we know of today.

CHAPTER 1:
STORIES FOR DECADES
A mini rant about inequality in storytelling

“The feminist utopian novel is a place where theories of power can be addressed through the construction of narratives that test and stretch the boundaries of power in its operational details.”(Bartkowski, Feminist Utopias, p. 5)

It’s not going to be possible to write about the significance of storytelling and how it is intertwined with the female experience, without mentioning Ursula K. Le Guin and her novel The Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction. Le Guin presents the idea that the stories we know of — and have been told numerous of times already — are the stories centered around adventures and heroes. Since the stories of prehistoric times, we have been fed with the story of the hunter going out on a dangerous adventure, defying the extreme weather, for then to come back home with the meat of his prey — and he will then be the man who survived to tell the story. Le Guin refers to it as The Killer Story The term ‘The Killer Story’ is used by Ursula K. Le Guin referring to the stories that revolves around heroes and the victory of surviving. These narratives are often neglecting the diverse perspectives from what could be referred to as ‘real people’.
and more importantly goes on to say “It isn’t their story. It’s his”. (Le Guin et al. The Carrier Bag of Fiction, p. 28)

The prehistoric human didn’t survive from hunting, but rather gathering, and in the end, the hunting was no necessity. But collecting oats and grains is harder to make interesting and probably less thrilling than listening to the tale of taking down a mammoth. This doesn’t mean that stories of the collector are any less important — far from. Le Guin argues that what we know, from this way of living, has taught us that the first manmade tool wasn’t a deathly weapon, but a container to collect and contain.

A container: a tool that can carry everything and be anything; from a womb carrying a child, to the woven basked storing the food that has been collected, to the novelle that holds the story, that then carries the words. To the words carrying knowledge.(Ibid) Words works like microorganisms, when they get planted the threads of them interconnects with each other, and by then starts to communicate and create kinship through patterns and entanglements in the greater narrative.

And never have I thought that I would find my Tupperware Tupperware is an American brand that manufactures plastic containers. The company was founded in 1942 by Earl Tupper. so poetic; look at me gazing at the food that has been shook in my bag and become one uniform, dense matter. I can store it and take it with me — I can move energy. Just like my words. Maybe to fully understand the female experience your first step is to enjoy and value the tool of the Tupperware?



On the other hand, the stories that have no heroes, but are filled up with real people, is by Le Guin being referred to as The Life Story Term used by Ursula K. Le Guin, referring to the stories that are filled with real people, rather than focusing on the hero or savior. Also argued that a novelle is a Life Story.
Which I, for the purpose of this thesis, translate into the story of the female experience — or maybe rather the lack of it.

“The society, the civilisation they were talking about, these theoreticians [Freud etc.], was evidently theirs; they owned it, they liked it; they were human, fully human, bashing, sticking, thrusting, killing. Wanting to be human too, I sought for evidence that I was; but if what it took, to make a weapon and kill with it, then evidently I was either extremely defective as a human, or not human at all…

…That’s right, they said. What you are is a woman.” (Ibid., p. 30-32).


This quote emphasises my allegation — that we are all born directly into a main narrative, prefabricated for only the hero, or the man, or the male experience, to succeed.

The very earliest documented stories are told to be explored in cave paintings, dated all the way back to the Paleolithic epoch, some would address this to be, relatively, in the very beginning. As already mentioned, during this time humans survived on gathered food.

But I’m guessing — unless you are highly interested in prehistoric times — that you might thought of spears, and men hunting mammals and other extinct wild animals. You are thinking of men running with deathly weapons and saving the day with meat to feast on. I’m not saying that you are a patriarch for having that assumption, but I’m saying, you, as many others, have become a victim of the patriarchal dominant master narrative — and to be fair, it’s hard not to be, since it is so deeply rooted within history. I, myself, didn’t think of the berry pickers and basket makers either.

To make an emphasis on the male-controlled — and navigated — narrative, I’m going to stay with the discourse of prehistoric times. It is said that the oldest find of cave art, mainly consist of depictions of the female sexual organ and various preys.(Viegas, "Female Sex Organs the Focus of the Oldest Known Cave Art") Professor R. Dale Guthrie speculates that cave art is a more personal outlet, and less about rituals or collective moments, that some professionals have suggested in other theories. Cave art is also believed to primarily be made by young men. Young men who experienced an increase in thoughts of their upcoming adult life, combined with some curiosity, which apparently — and not hard to imagine when you think about the stereotypical gender roles — led them to have nothing else on their mind than sex and hunting and suddenly the cave art turned into a “…Paleolithic playboy magazine”.(Gray, "Cave Art and the Evolution of the Human Mind", p. 62)

Even prehistoric times prove to tap into the male gaze A feministic theory that describes the act of sexualizing women for the satisfaction of the cis male viewer, which can cause women to be objectified. And for men to believe that the female body and mind is something to possess and conquer for them to become successful. Term is mostly used in the realm of visual arts and literature and is strongly related to the patriarchy.
andtells us a story that (most) men have been so comfortable within the narrative, that they don’t have the need to progress — though they did evolve from the stages of cave art. Or did they?

In my humble opinion it still seems like the male gender use their reproductive organs as a moral compass to navigate through the world.

If you need a scapegoat for this theory, one could be Plato. Plato understood the importance of storytelling, which for the concern of this thesis feels a bit contradictory. In the book Republic Republic is a series of dialogues between Socrates and other important male figures. The publication is narrated by Plato. The dialogues are mostly concerned about political theory, justice, and the ideal man.
it’s discussed how to use and moderate storytelling, in the benefit of the political goal and power. And further on the perception, that the tool of stories and myths should only concern Gods and heroes, due to the idea that people need aspiration and a form of image to the perfect. By having this they would have a clear vision on what was thought on to be the ideal man, the hero, which then can be imitated.(Durec, Shaping Imaginations: Plato's Republic on Stories and Storytelling) So you might not like the fascist approach of controlling the narrative, but it’s another argument, why storytelling is such a powerful tool.

By now the narrative of this thesis is starting to become a very one-sided way of storytelling. Storytelling is of course more than the retelling of the past or read about it in found books. With the development of how society are structured today, especially with all the medias, storytelling is way more diversified, and stories are mediated through everything from bedtime stories, images (still and moving), activism, to even social platforms.(Sontag and Berger, To Tell a Story) Stories aren’t universal in the sense that we alle share the same story or perception of it, our perception are shaped by our individual experiences which is why we never will reach a perfect common ground on how we read and understand the world — or the master narrative. I believe it’s safe to assume that we are all familiar with storytelling as a tool. A tool of learning. To learn about the past, cultures, and our social surroundings. But we might be less familiar with the idea of using storytelling to change the master narrative — or the world so to say.

According to Aristotle a narrative connects events, it doesn’t matter if the narrative follows a classic pattern of beginning, middle, ending or being non-linear — a narrative will always resemble a journey. (Le Guin, Dancing at the Edge of the World: Thoughts on Words, Women, Places) Stories, especially the ones that stems from oral tradition, are peoples own description of themselves and their way of facing life. These descriptions often differ from descriptions made by professionals such as sociologists, scientists, or anthropologists.

And even though there might be a slight distortion, or untruth in people’s self-image through storytelling, there is no need to believe that a professional who studies culture through their own perception of life, should have a more truthful image of another person and their culture.(Bronner, "Folklore as a Mirror of Culture") That idea becomes more and more absurd the more you think about it, due to the gap there is between reality and perception, a gap that allows interfering and creates room for change.(Tiffee, "Mind the Gap Between Perception and Reality") The question is how to rewrite the story, how to detangle the master narrative for then to weave in new and unheard voices that didn’t get the change to affect the narrative.

CHAPTER 2: TO MINE THE GAPS

“To assume the tendentious voice of utopian projections takes a certain amount of confidence and sense of power to imagine collective change.”(Bartkowski, Feminist Utopias, p. 9)

By now you must have realised, that equality simply does not exist, at least not yet. By just stating this creates yet another gap, for you to either oppose or agree, or maybe be a bit more nuanced. For now, I rely on, that you can agree with me without too many questions — it would simply take up too much of the collected word count to explain. But what I will do is to put the statement into the context of storytelling and fiction.

The individual human will always be its own protagonist, our perception of challenges and unfairness of the world are defined by our own personal standpoint, we can’t experience what others are experiencing. Neither can we fully experience what have already happened. The only thing to we can do is to navigate in the aftermath of it and explore the results of how the story has played out so far. There is a duality in this that is challenging for very different reasons (1) It takes great courage to share a story, even if it doesn’t contain any trauma or violence. But maybe even the smallest urge to share a story, means that no one else has done it yet, and that can feel lonesome. (2) To be the receiver and to fully listen and reflect upon it is difficult. As mentioned, you can’t fully experience other people’s experiences, and this can lead the recipient to fully miscomprehend.

But, to somehow get closer to full equality, we must practice both parameters of it. Imagine a world where it has been led by people who fully understood the female experience — we might wouldn’t even had to talk about it by now.

Being a woman in this time era and I’m not even aware if we are in the beginning, middle or at the end of the story — at times, it feels like we are getting closer to the end. Some argue that this epoch we live in at this time, is the Anthropocene. The term Anthropocene was coined by Paul Crutzen in 2000.
(Haraway, "Tentacular Thinking: Anthropocene, Capitalocene, Chthulucene.") The term mainly refers to climate and environmental changes that — due to human impact — are way pass irreversible. It has this doomsday notion and suddenly I’m thinking the rhetoric about this epoch is why it feels like we are getting closer to the end. If the world ended right now it would be incredibly sad, for many reasons, one being that the narrative still is dominated by The Killer Story and there would be no time to heal the past or change the future. “There are so many good stories yet to tell, so many netbags yet to string, and not just by human beings.”(Ibid.)

However, in more recent times the term Capitalocene The term Capitalocene was coined by Andreas Malm in 2009.
has been suggested and it is argued that we should not blame the individual human but see the alarming climate changes more as a systematic flaw, also known as capitalism. And just by a change of epoch we prove that we just not yet are at the very end.

Here are three good reasons why NOT to use the term Anthropocene by Donna Haraway:

"(1) The myth system associated with the Anthropos is a setup, and the stories end badly. More to the point, they end in double death; they are not about ongoingness. It is hard to tell a good story with such a bad actor. Bad actors need a story, but not the whole story.

(2) Species Man does not make history.

(3) Man plus Tool does not make history. That is the story of History human exceptionalists tell.”(Ibid.)

So, for the sake of ongoingness and the Capitalocene, I’m going to drift the focus back to The Life Story. The story that has been put in an airtight Tupperware, contained, and for way too long. Placed in the back of the fridge and now, no one dares to open it anymore. Too terrified to be hit in the face with a pungent smell that triggers something uncomfortable — and why put yourself in that situation? What really is going to happen — if you are brave enough to open the lid — is that the oxygen will feed the story and the energy will make the threads of it grow. It will then become bigger and spread wider than before.

The author Saidiya Hartman refers to the notion of opening the lid, or mining the gap, as critical fabulation. A term that describes a creative method of combining history with fiction, and to explore the narrative that already took place.(Okeowo, "How Saidiya Hartman Retells the History of Black Life") Hartman’s essay Venus in Two Acts is a story of two enslaved black girls. The fragments that Hartman uses are from the archive of Atlantic slavery which consists of no more than legal documents, protocols and account books stating their death and identifying them as nothing more than numbers.(Hartman, "Venus in Two Acts") When Hartman starts to read the archives, she opens the lid of the container, and by doing so being faced with the position of the archive, institutionalised, powerful and by that being the curator of the knowledge of the past. Due to this notion Hartman was urged to write a counter-story, a story that empowered the unheard voices in the narrative. In this case, the story of the two enslaved girls. (Ibid.) But how do we place ourselves in the act of telling other people’s stories?

Hartman herself questions “How can narrative embody life in words and at the same time respect what we cannot know?”.(Ibid. p. 14) She also poses the indication that the fiction and writing from her, is not about filling in the gaps and create a new master narrative. But rather to create a fuller picture of the life of the girls, to focus on The Life Story and not to make an uplifting heroine story about slavery, but to make sure that words from a slave holds the same value in the greater narrative, as a scholarly philosopher. (Okeowo, "How saidiya Hartman Retells the History of Black Life")

Critical fabulation is essentially a creative act of reading into an archive — or multiple — collecting the fragments of spoken objects and documents, and by then create a narrative exploring the unwanted voices of the story. To practice this act, you will need an archive.

The traditional concept of an archive is explained by Jacques Derrida to be rooted in the Greek antiquity and had close connection with the power and politics of the community.(Derrida and Prenowitz, "Archive Fever: A Freudian Impression") This concept, is to this day still the building blocks of the traditional archives The idea of the traditional archives is meant to be read as, archives that are situated and rooted in physical space and focusing on keeping, and storing, information on historically collections about cultures.
— the archives that we in contemporary times tend to rely on for seeking answers of the past. The idea of this kind of archive is a significant factor that should be taken into consideration when discussing archived material and the inclusivity of it.

Further on Derrida opens to the complexity of the physical space of the archive, and how this has evolved simultaneously with the development of society and technology.(Ibid.)

And with that being the starting point in the discussion of the definition and the role of the archive in storytelling, I want to challenge the idea of the archive and what it can be. Could it be the mouldy bread or maybe the unopened Tupperware?

CHAPTER 3: The GYNOCENE AND ALL THE WOMEN I'M SUPPOSED TO BE

My personal archive, my container, my Tupperware, is filled with my personal experiences. It contains conversations, relations, movies, images, travels, objects, smells, music — the list could go on and on. Some might refer to this as a collection of memories. I see them more as ingredients. My archive doesn’t have a system, everything gets thrown into the same container with no compartments, just one big space where everything sloshes around and tangles up — growing in and out of each other, creating kinship. Every now and then I open the container, just to give it some oxygen to feed upon, but I never let it out — it could accidentally get out of control — so I constrain myself and let the collection of experiences ferment in peace. And why? You might think?

Joanna Russ once wrote a letter to feminist literary historian and anthologist Susan H. Koppelman, questioning and critiquing another feminist “Where is her anger? I think from now on, I will not trust anyone who isn’t angry.”(Russ, To Write Like a Woman: Essays in Feminism and Science Fiction)

Female authors have for many years been suppressed in the field of writing.(Russ, How to Suppress Women’s Writing) — well in general, the female experience has been expressed through all kinds of media as being rather difficult — returning to history, the female experience have been automatically devaluated and considered to be more narrow than the male experience, the master narrative has been to be absorbed by men and their God/hero complex Coming back to Plato and the concept of moderating the stories, The God/hero complex is meant to be understood as the idea of when the male experience is becoming superior to the female experience, and by then creating a bigger gap in the equality between all.
this notion “…allows men to not experience female suffering as representative of human — and therefore male — suffering. Female suffering is… less pertinent, less significant, less threatening than the pain which befalls men.”(Chesler, About Men, p. 205) Joanna Russ, mostly known for her writings in the genre of science fiction, has written a (novelle) where she with sarcasm — and deeply rooted anger and frustration — maps out a tool of analytics to acknowledge the patterns of suppression, in the field of writing. The cover of the book plastered with quotes that all begins with “She wrote it, but…”(Russ, How to Suppress Women’s Writing) suggests that Russ is only referring to the writing of women.

In the prologue Russ makes use of the tools of SF SF, science fiction, speculative fiction, speculative feminism. and writes about the Glotologs White cis men. — being an intergalactic creature, judging what and who can make art, and how to keep certain groups away from the art “In normally egalitarian society the ideal situation (socially speaking) is one in which the members of the ‘wrong’ groups have the freedom to engage in literature (or equally significant activities) and yet do not do so, thus proving that they can’t.” (Russ, How to Suppress Women’s Writing, p. 4)

The tools of Russ’ analytic toolkit can also be applied to other forms of arts than just writing, and with the power of storytelling being incredible diversified, it seems fitting to look into how storytelling has been used in other ways. In activism where the fight is often between a them and us. It is important to understand the power of the tools and how you can utilise them to your benefit. Ask yourself who do you want to listen to you? This van become the difference between successfully get your message through or not.

Guerilla Girls is an activistic group who actively make use of tools in their communication, some of them that resembles the same tools we acknowledge being used in fiction. As an example, their decision to use physical gorilla masks, which does not only provide them privacy, but helps them to emphasise the issue of their activism, instead of focusing on their personal character. But also, the mask, provides a humoristic aspect to their campaigning, that involves people more.(Chadwick and Guerrilla Girls, Confessions of the Guerrilla Girls) This also suggest that humour is being used as a tool to get rid of the (at the time) notion that feminists didn’t have any sense of fun and being a rather stuck up group of people. Even though the use of gorilla mask seems as a simple gesture, it can be interpreted in so many ways, Guerilla Girls themselves have stated, that it also made them step into a line of, traditionally male and masked heroes, such as Robin, Batman and Spiderman. In their own words this helped them gain some sort of “mask-ulinity.”(Ibid. p. 15)

And yet again we are witnessing the unwanted voices of our society to be experts in the act of shapeshifting. Indirectly asked to constantly analyse the room and figure out how to fit in, while the room never take shape after us, and if after all if it does happen it is with a resistantly attitude and with an expectation of a sincere and praised thank you. I’m like a scoby, a Vinegar Mother, feed me with oxygen and sugar to keep me alive. I will only take the room you offer. My experience are the ingredients that nurtures me and make sure I grow — and the world is my container. But what you don’t know, is what happens if you don’t take care of me. If you forget about me and keep me contained in the dark for too long, the process of turning sugar into anger will start. A chemical process that can’t be stopped and the longer you wait to check up on me, the more my anger will ferment. Like gas, it will infiltrate every memory and experience, becoming paramount and if you ever decide to open the lid, I will for sure explode.

Hopefully we are looking into another epoch, for the sake of the changes that comes with it. I just read an essay that suggests that the next era in time should be named the Gynocene.(Van Der Bly, "Welcome to the Gynocene") A suggestion that refers to the inequality between genders and how the all the epochs we have lived so far, have been shaped by male figures — Glotologs — or just them. Martha Van Der Bly suggests that the earth can’t be healed unless the inequality between male and unwanted voices are healed. (Ibid.)

But how to do so, and how can stories contribute to the healing of this inequality? Le Guin refers to the power of language and how we in school are taught the language spoken by white men — also referred to as father tongue. It is a useful language that communicates power and knowledge, but also spoken from above with no answer expected “White man speak dichotomy”(Ursula K. Le Guin, Bryn Mawr Commencement Address) and creates the gaps, this language decides what is right and what is wrong and emphasise the notion of us and them. On the other hand, there is mother tongue, the language of connection, relations and emotions, the language that goes two ways while entangling itself in the web of sharing and listening. Mother tongue is the language that stories are spoken in, and offers experiences as being the truth. Le Guin continues and emphasises the importance of understanding the power of both the languages(Ibid.), by understanding how to make use of both the tongues, the gap of inequality might slowly start to close up. It’s like an alchemical marriage between the sun and the moon as soon as they become a union the balance will be restored — or at least that the idea.

Coming back to how this relates to storytelling and the female experience is to stress power the story holds, even it being fiction. Focusing on the written aspect of storytelling I am going to refer to yet another female author and her perspective on why she writes. Joan Didion describes writing as an act of saying I, to share experience and invade the reader’s most intimate space. “In many ways, writing is the act of saying I, of imposing oneself upon other people, of saying listen to me, see it my way, change your mind. It’s an aggressive, even a hostile act.”(Didion, "Why I Write”) And with this amazing quote I’m going to stop the invasion of your intimate space, give you a rest and some space to breath and reflect on how your story fits into the master narrative of the world.

TO CONCLUDE

The analogy of mould in a loaf of bread captures the nature of storytelling. Just like an airborne fungus, that with an immense speed, travel through the gaps of air in the porous body of the bread — stories that are spoken, written, and brought to life acts the same. As soon as the stories comes to life, they will immediately infiltrate the gaps of the untold and create entanglements with the master narrative of the world. Sometimes the strings of the stories are going to be visible and other times most of us won’t even notice the newly created web in the narrative.

Ursula K. Le Guin’s The Carrier Bag of Fiction speaks of the notion of the first manmade tool being a container, rather than a weapon, and continues with expanding the idea of what a container is. Le Guin argues that the novelle also can be considered a container, that holds the power of words. The novelle diviates from the idea that every story needs a hero, but the world are in need of the stories told in mother tongue and with real people, also referred to as The Life Story. With a point of departure in this notion the thesis continously explores different ways of using storytelling as a tool of healing the master narrative. Saydiya Hartman’s critcal fabulation are using the fragmented archives to mine the gaps of the history and by use of tools of fiction, Hartman are giving back the voices to the oppressed of the history. Or with Guerilla Girls who are making use of gorilla masks in order to be more accomodating to the outside world, and hopefully removing the idea of feminist being up tight.

As we entangle ourselves into the speculative realm, contemplating the next epoch as suggested by Martha Van Der Bly–the Gynocene–a era where women, shapeshifters, and other unwanted voices are not only living in the world made by men, but are a part of creating the world. The suggestion is that the Earth can only heal when the inequality between male and unwanted voices is addressed opens the door to the transformative potential of storytelling.

Through this exploring I have been actively searching for argument that contained the importance of storytelling and fiction, and how this is dominated by The Killer Story. Through my research and writing I have become increasable more and more frustrate, every new knowledge and text, was getting thrown into my personal archive and by laying around there tangled up in each other, my research started to grow and ferment. The result of this process is this thesis, that with a great personal angle, maps out the use of storytelling, language and novelle and how I since the beginning of human life, have been used to shape the world.
